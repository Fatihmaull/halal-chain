\subsection{Blockchain Supply Chain Traceability}
Systematic reviews identify immutability, auditability, and multi-stakeholder trust as primary motivations for blockchain in supply chains~\cite{casino2021systematic,kshetri2018blockchain,galvez2018future}.
Permissioned ledgers (Hyperledger Fabric) dominate enterprise pilots because they offer identity management, private channels, and predictable throughput~\cite{andoni2019blockchain,abeyratne2016blockchain}.
Public chains enable permissionless read access---any consumer with a batch identifier can verify status without vendor cooperation---at the cost of transaction fees and wallet onboarding for writers~\cite{underwood2016blockchain}.
Layer~2 rollups such as Optimistic rollups (including the OP Stack underlying Base) batch transactions to L1 while inheriting Ethereum security assumptions, reducing per-operation cost~\cite{kalodner2018arbitrum,optimism2024spec}.
HalalChain adopts a public L2 precisely to preserve read transparency for consumers while keeping write costs within UMKM reach.

Food-industry reviews note recurring patterns: on-chain hashes or identifiers, off-chain payloads, and event logs for provenance~\cite{rejeb2020blockchain,kouhizadeh2018blockchain}.
Caro \emph{et al.} implement agri-food traceability with blockchain-backed event chains~\cite{caro2018blockchain}; HalalChain differs by centering \emph{certification status} and \emph{auditor decisions} rather than logistics events alone.

\subsection{Halal Integrity Systems}
Halal supply chain literature emphasizes traceability from farm to fork, certification authenticity, and cross-border recognition~\cite{sidarto2021halal,rejeb2018halal,rashid2018halal}.
Sidarto and Hamka propose blockchain for poultry traceability in Indonesia, highlighting integration challenges with existing BPJPH processes~\cite{sidarto2021halal}.
Rejeb \emph{et al.} combine HACCP, blockchain, and IoT for halal meat supply chains~\cite{rejeb2018halal}.
Recent proposals combine blockchain with QR verification and AI-assisted document checks~\cite{alourani2025halal,marianingsih2024halal}.

However, few systems model \emph{auditor rejection} as a terminal on-chain state with a structured resubmission path.
In practice, rejected applications are corrected off-ledger and re-enter workflows opaquely---undermining accountability (\emph{Hisba}).
HalalChain encodes rejection reasons, audit document CIDs, and \texttt{parentBatchId} links so that consumers and regulators can inspect the full decision trail.

\subsection{Off-Chain Storage and IPFS}
Storing full PDF certificates and lab reports on-chain is economically infeasible: gas scales with calldata size, and chain nodes replicate all data globally~\cite{benet2014ipfs,christidis2016blockchain}.
Content-addressable storage via IPFS derives identifiers (CIDs) from document hashes; any tampering changes the CID, enabling integrity checks when the CID is anchored on-chain~\cite{benet2014ipfs}.
Christidis and Devetsikiotis survey smart-contract and IoT integration patterns where blockchains store references, not payloads~\cite{christidis2016blockchain}.
HalalChain follows this pattern: the contract stores \texttt{ipfsCid} and \texttt{auditIpfsCid} strings; Pinata provides pinning so gateways remain resolvable during evaluation.

\subsection{Research Gap}
Existing halal digital systems predominantly rely on centralized databases or enterprise blockchains without wallet-free consumer verification.
HalalChain combines: (i)~public L2 verifiability; (ii)~RBAC-separated producer/auditor roles; (iii)~immutable revision history after rejection; and (iv)~explicit mapping of ethical design requirements to protocol properties (Section~\ref{sec:prelim}).
Published L2 gas benchmarks from prior work~\cite{kalodner2018arbitrum} inform cost motivation; HalalChain-specific mainnet or testnet metrics are reported only after deployment (Section~\ref{sec:eval-b}).
