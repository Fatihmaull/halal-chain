\subsection{Blockchain Supply Chain Traceability}
Systematic reviews identify immutability, auditability, and multi-stakeholder trust as primary motivations for blockchain in supply chains~\cite{casino2021systematic,kshetri2018blockchain,galvez2018future}.
Permissioned ledgers (Hyperledger Fabric) dominate enterprise pilots~\cite{andoni2019blockchain}, while public chains enable permissionless consumer verification at the cost of gas fees---motivating Layer~2 deployment~\cite{kalodner2018arbitrum,optimism2024spec}.

\subsection{Halal Integrity Systems}
Halal supply chain literature emphasizes traceability from farm to fork and certification authenticity~\cite{sidarto2021halal,rejeb2018halal,rashid2018halal}.
Recent proposals combine blockchain with QR verification for halal products~\cite{alourani2025halal,marianingsih2024halal}, but few address auditor rejection workflows with immutable revision history on a public L2.

\subsection{Off-Chain Storage and IPFS}
Storing full documents on-chain is economically infeasible; content-addressable off-chain storage via IPFS with on-chain content identifier (CID) anchoring is a established pattern~\cite{benet2014ipfs,christidis2016blockchain,caro2018blockchain}.

\subsection{Research Gap}
Existing halal digital systems predominantly rely on centralized databases.
HalalChain combines public verifiability, L2 cost efficiency, auditor-driven workflow, and revision history---while explicitly mapping ethical design requirements to enforceable protocol properties (Section~\ref{sec:prelim}).
Published L2 gas benchmarks from prior work~\cite{kalodner2018arbitrum} inform cost motivation; HalalChain-specific metrics are reported only after testnet deployment (Section~\ref{sec:eval-b}).
