\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Trust Assumptions and Limitations}
We explicitly state the following trust assumptions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Administrator honesty} --- \texttt{DEFAULT\_ADMIN\_ROLE} grants auditor privileges; compromise of the admin key compromises auditor integrity.
  \item \textbf{Auditor diligence} --- on-chain records attest that an auditor address approved a batch; they do not cryptographically prove halal compliance of ingredients.
  \item \textbf{IPFS pinning availability} --- documents require active Pinata pins; unpinned CIDs become unavailable off-chain while on-chain CIDs remain.
  \item \textbf{Testnet scope} --- evaluation on Base Sepolia does not imply production BPJPH integration or mainnet readiness.
\end{enumerate}

These limitations partially constrain the \emph{Adl} (justice) design requirement until multi-signature auditor governance is implemented~\cite{gnosis2024safe}.

\subsection{Comparison with Centralized Alternatives}
Centralized databases offer lower operational complexity but permit retroactive record alteration by administrators~\cite{bendhaou2021blockchain}.
HalalChain trades this for public verifiability and immutable audit trails at the cost of wallet onboarding for producers/auditors and IPFS infrastructure.

\subsection{Future Work}
Future directions include multi-signature auditor verification, DAO-based auditor election, Base mainnet deployment, IoT sensor anchoring, and integration with BPJPH SIHALAL if APIs become available.
