\begin{abstract}
The global halal economy exceeds USD~2 trillion in consumer spending, yet certification trust remains fragile: physical certificates can be forged, enterprise databases can be altered by privileged administrators, and consumers rarely obtain batch-level proof at the point of purchase.
Indonesia's mandatory halal labeling regime (BPJPH/MUI) intensifies the need for verifiable, low-cost traceability for micro, small, and medium enterprises (UMKM) that cannot afford enterprise blockchain platforms.
This paper presents \emph{HalalChain}, a decentralized batch traceability protocol deployed on Base Network---an Ethereum Layer~2 rollup---that anchors certification \emph{status}, content identifiers (CIDs), and audit metadata on a public ledger while storing supporting documents (PDFs, lab reports, ingredient lists) on the InterPlanetary File System (IPFS).
We contribute: (i)~a minimal three-role smart contract design with OpenZeppelin role-based access control (RBAC) and immutable revision history after auditor rejection; (ii)~an explicit mapping from Maqasid-aligned ethical \emph{design requirements} to enforceable protocol properties; (iii)~an open-source implementation comprising Solidity contracts, Hardhat tests, and bilingual producer/auditor/consumer web dashboards with wallet-free consumer QR verification.
Section~\ref{sec:eval} reports qualitative security analysis and functional scenario testing; on Ethereum Sepolia testnet, \texttt{registerBatch} used 155,506 gas (0.00078367 ETH) and \texttt{verifyBatch} used 79,214 gas (0.00034832 ETH).
HalalChain demonstrates that halal batch integrity can be made publicly auditable without requiring consumers to operate cryptocurrency wallets.
A live prototype is available at \url{https://web-six-ivory-36.vercel.app/}.
\end{abstract}
